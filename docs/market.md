# SeniorBrowse — Key Marketing Insights

## 1. Product Success Definition

A senior independently reads news and weather — without caregiver help.
This is the only metric that matters at MVP stage.

---

## 2. Customer Profile (ICP)

### Who buys (decision maker)
- Family caregiver — typically son/daughter, age 35–45
- Professional caregiver managing multiple seniors
- Institutions: Senior Academies, care homes

### Who uses (end user)
- Senior aged 60–80+, has an old family hand-me-down computer/laptop
- Large screen is an advantage over tablets — easier to operate
- Barrier: lack of confidence to try, small icons, discouragement after failure

### Key observation
Families most often choose option C — **they give up** and accept that the senior doesn't use the internet.
They are not actively looking for a solution. This means there is no active demand — the product must find the caregiver, not the other way around.

---

## 3. Positioning

### What SeniorBrowse is NOT
- Not another adblocker
- Not parental control software (stigmatising for seniors)
- Not a new browser (installation barrier)

### What SeniorBrowse IS
The only extension that combines three things in one:
1. UX designed for seniors (large icons, simple navigation)
2. Caregiver panel for configuration — no technical knowledge required
3. Security built in by default

### Message for the caregiver
> "Set it up once — mom browses on her own."

### Message for the senior
> "The internet without fear. Only what you love."

---

## 4. Market Gaps (Opportunities)

- No one has combined a caregiver panel + senior UX in a single extension
- Existing tools require installing a new browser or are designed for children
- The Polish market is practically unserved in this segment
- Seniors with laptops/PCs are a niche that tablets and phones don't serve well

---

## 5. Competition — What's Missing

| Solution | What it does well | What's missing |
|---|---|---|
| Accessible Browser | Large buttons, simple UI | Requires new browser install, dead project |
| uBlock / AdBlock | Blocks ads | Zero senior UX, no navigation |
| Parental controls | Caregiver panel | UI built for children, stigmatising |
| Windows Narrator/Focus | Built-in, free | Complex setup, no dedicated web navigation |

---

## 6. Acquisition Channels — Recommendations

### B2C (longer acquisition cycle)
- Facebook — family caregiver groups, local community groups
- YouTube — tutorials like "how to help mom use the internet"
- Word-of-mouth — one satisfied caregiver brings several more

### B2B (shorter cycle, larger contract)
- Senior Academies — ready distribution channel, actively looking for tools
- Care homes — one contract = many users
- Digital inclusion organisations for seniors

### Priority
B2B should be tested in parallel with B2C — one conversation with a Senior Academy will provide more validation than 100 downloads from the Chrome Web Store.

---

## 7. Marketing Risks

1. **No active demand** — caregivers are not searching for this product. Awareness of the problem must be built before selling the solution.

2. **Installation barrier** — an extension requires someone technical. Without simple onboarding (wizard "set up in 3 minutes") the product never reaches the senior.

3. **Option C as the norm** — most families have accepted digital exclusion of their senior. Marketing must break this mindset, not just describe the product.

4. **No B2B validation** — no contacts at Senior Academies or care homes yet. This is the biggest unknown before scaling.

5. **Monetisation is secondary** — charitable project, but without a financial model it may quietly die when motivation runs out.

---

## 8. MVP — What to Communicate at Launch

Two features, one message:
- **Home page with shortcuts** — senior clicks large icons, goes where they want
- **Side navigation panel** — always visible, large Back/Home/Zoom buttons

Everything else (drag-and-drop, profiles, history, cloud) — after first users.
